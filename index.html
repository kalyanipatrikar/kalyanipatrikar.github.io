Graph Neural Network Predicting Molecular Properties

Ground state and excited state energies can be calculated from molecular structure to a high degree of accuracy by first principles methods such as those based on density functional theory, but such methods are computationally expensive. Various aspects of molecular structure such as atomic composition, bond order, or aromaticity combine in complex ways to determine its properties. The correlation of structural features and molecular energy is not obvious.

We present a graph attention network that correlates molecular energy with molecular structure. It requires minimal structural features as input, and is demonstrated to be more accurate than linear regression methods or graph neural networks.

Origin of Contact Resistance in Organic Devices

Organic devices rely on charge injection from typically inorganic electrodes for operation. However, interfaces of organic and inorganic materials are resistive to charge transfer, due to mismatch of energy levels, defects, and orbital interactionns. However details of the mechanism is uncertain.

First principles simulations of interfaces of different organic semiconductors and electrodes revealed that a combination of energy levels of polymer and electrode, along with strength of electronic coupling between their orbitals and states determined the contact resistance. Rate of charge transfer was calculated based on computed values, and shown to correlate with measured contact resistance of transistors; thus uncovering the mechanism as well as providing a computational model evaluating electrode materials for organic devices.

Mechanisms of Self Assembled Monolayer at Device Interfaces

Experimental studies and first pricniples computations are coimbined to reveal how self assembled monolayers modulate charge transport at device interfaces. Subsequently, device model and analytical model are developed that correlate properties of the SAM molecules and interface, with the resulting reduction in contact resistance.

Molecular Design for Rapid Charge Transport

Mobility of organic semiconductors depend on various factors, including molecular structure, intermolecular interactions, and energetic disorder in the material. We find two key ways to enhance mobility by molecular design. We showed that appropriate side chain substituents can lower the reorganisation energy of molecule and hence increase mobility. Further we showed that rather than energetic disorder, charge transfer integral at homo-junctions played a more crucial role in detrmining the charge transport in donor-acceptor co-polymers, thus establishing guidelines for molecular design.
